I'm freshinig up my statistics knowledge, and trying to make a better sense of the [Central Limit Theorem (CLT)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_limit_theorem). There are quite a few derivations out there, and there is a great in depth post about it by Terence Tao [here](https://terrytao.wordpress.com/2010/01/05/254a-notes-2-the-central-limit-theorem/).
Yet, I found this very neat derivation, that I think appealed to my inner physicsist, due to it's resemblnce to technics used in quantum field theory and statistical physics.


## Prelims
#### The distribution of a statistic 
Given a random sample $\boldsymbol{X}=\\{X_i\\}_{i=1}^N $, the random variable $ Z=T(\boldsymbol{X}) $ is called a *statistic*. The distribuion of $Z$ is most easily calculated through its CDF

$$ F_Z(z)={\cal P}(Z>z)={\cal P}(T(\boldsymbol{X})>z)=\int d\boldsymbol{X} \Theta(T(\boldsymbol{X})-z)f_{\boldsymbol{X}}(\boldsymbol{X}),$$


where $f_{\boldsymbol{X}}(\boldsymbol{X})$ is the joint ditribution of $\boldsymbol{X}$ and $\Theta$ is the Heavyside step function. Since the PDF is the derivative of the  CDF, we can write

$$ f_Z(z)=\frac{d}{dz}F_Z(z)=\int d\boldsymbol{X} \delta(T(\boldsymbol{X})-z)f_{\boldsymbol{X}}(\boldsymbol{X}), $$

where \\(\delta\\) is the Dirac delta function.


#### The distribution of the sum over of a samle
Next we define the random variable $S=\sum_{i=1}^N X_i$. The distribution of $S$ is given by

$$ f_S(s)=\int d\boldsymbol{X} \delta(s-S)f_{\boldsymbol{X}}(\boldsymbol{X}). $$
